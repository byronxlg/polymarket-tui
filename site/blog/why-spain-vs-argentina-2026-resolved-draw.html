Why Spain vs Argentina 2026 resolved Draw on Polymarket

2026-07-20/5 min read/market report

Spain won the 2026 World Cup on Sunday, beating Argentina 1-0 with a Ferran Torres goal in the 106th minute. On Polymarket, "Will Spain win on 2026-07-19?" resolved No - and "Will Spain vs. Argentina end in a draw?" resolved Yes. Nothing malfunctioned. The final split its markets exactly the way the resolution rules said it would, and the price history is a minute-by-minute record of it happening. Prices below were fetched from the Gamma and CLOB APIs on 2026-07-20 at 19:47 UTC; the resolved ones are final.

01The scoreboard vs the ledger

One match, four resolutions

The final at MetLife Stadium finished 0-0 after 90 minutes. Argentina's Enzo Fernández was sent off with a second yellow in second-half stoppage time, and Spain scored the only goal early in the second period of extra time - Nico Williams finding Torres in the 106th minute - to win its second men's World Cup. Here is how Polymarket settled the event:

WORLD CUP FINAL, JULY 19                        RESOLVED
Will Spain win the 2026 FIFA World Cup?         Yes
Will Spain win on 2026-07-19?                   No
Will Spain vs. Argentina end in a draw?         Yes
Will Argentina win on 2026-07-19?               No

A team that lifted the trophy while its own match market resolved No looks like a contradiction until you read one sentence of the rules.

02The sentence that decided it

Match markets end at the 90-minute whistle

The resolution text of each match market carries the clause: it "refers only to the outcome within the first 90 minutes of regular play plus stoppage time." Extra time and penalties do not exist for these markets. The championship market, by contrast, pays whoever ends up holding the trophy, however long that takes.

So when the referee ended regulation at 0-0, three markets were already decided: Draw resolved Yes, and both team match markets resolved No - while the championship market kept trading through extra time as the only live question left. This is not a Polymarket quirk; it mirrors how 90-minute match odds work across sports books. But on an exchange the rule is a paragraph of text on the market page, and the price only makes sense if you have read it (see what a Polymarket price means).

03The repricing, minute by minute

32c to 100c, the slow way

The chart below shows Spain's championship price and the draw market through the match window (10-minute prints, times UTC):

The draw's line is the story of a goalless match told in cents. It opened the day at 32c - the pre-match price of "nobody scores enough in 90" - and every scoreless minute pushed it higher: 41c around half time, 53c by the 20:50 print, 90c deep in stoppage. Within a minute of the full-time whistle it printed 99.9c and was done. The two-minute prints catch one wobble on the way: a dip from 90c to 86c around 21:14, seconds of doubt about whether something had happened in stoppage time, then straight to settled.

Spain's championship line is subtler. It barely moved through 90 minutes - a goalless match was already its base case - then rose to 70c as regulation closed with Argentina down to ten men. From there it drifted back to 60c as extra time wore on, the market pricing an approaching penalty shootout as close to a coin flip. The Torres goal snapped it from 60c to 94c between the 21:42 and 21:44 prints. Even then the market held back six cents of doubt with a quarter hour plus stoppage to play; 100c only printed after the final whistle.

All of this happened on real size. The API's trailing 24-hour volume figures at fetch time - a window that only opened midway through the first half - still showed $11.3M traded on the Spain match market, $7.1M on Argentina, and $4.2M on the draw.

04The gap that called it

The pre-match prices already contained this ending

Three days before the final, our pre-match post walked through an odd-looking pair of numbers: Spain was 58.6c to be champion but only 42.5c to win the match, with the draw at 31.5c. The 16c gap between Spain's two prices was the extra-time scenario - the market pricing a final that goes past 90 minutes as roughly a coin flip that leans Spain.

That is the scenario that happened, and the in-play prices stayed consistent with the pre-match read to the end: when regulation finished 0-0, Spain's championship price sat in the 60s - a coin flip's 50c plus a lean for the team with an extra man and the run of play. The market did not predict the ending. It priced the paths to every ending, and the path the match took was one it had already measured at about a one-in-three chance.

05The market still open

The World Cup is over; the trading is not

One final-adjacent market was still live when these numbers were fetched: "Will Trump be in the WC Champions Photo?" - Yes at 99.35c with $4.87M traded in 24 hours, a day after the match. Its rules require Trump to be "visibly in the frame" of the winning team's official photo, resolved by a consensus of credible reporting, and until that consensus is documented the market keeps trading. Even at 99c, the last cent is a resolution-rules question, not a football question. Reading the rules is not a beginner tip; it is the whole game.
